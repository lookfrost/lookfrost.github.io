Why Linux customization still matters to me

Not because it looks cool, but because it changes the relationship with the machine

What pulled me into Linux was never just the visual side. The interesting part was control. You start realizing that the computer does not have to stay inside the shape someone else gave it. You can change the workflow, the feel, the behavior, and sometimes the whole philosophy of how it works.

I have spent time across a lot of desktop environments and window managers, from full desktops to lighter setups. Some were clean, some were chaotic, some were overhyped, and some quietly worked better than expected. That kind of experimentation teaches you taste, but it also teaches you systems thinking.

Customization is not superficial

A good setup reduces friction. It makes common actions faster, makes the machine clearer to read, and gives you a stronger sense of ownership. When something is truly yours, you notice problems sooner and care more about solving them properly.

Why I still keep coming back to it

Even when I use Windows for practical reasons, the Linux mindset stays with me. I still care about how a system is structured, how configurable it is, what assumptions it makes, and whether it respects the person using it. That mindset carries over into everything else I build or troubleshoot.
