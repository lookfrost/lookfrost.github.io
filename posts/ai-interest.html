Why AI got me this hooked in the first place

The moment software stopped feeling passive

What pulled me into AI was not the marketing around it and not the idea of replacing everything with a chatbot. The interesting part was much simpler: software suddenly felt less passive. Instead of clicking through a rigid tool, you could describe what you wanted, iterate on it, and sometimes get something genuinely useful back.

That changes the relationship completely. A normal tool waits for instructions in a fixed shape. AI systems can help you think through a task, compress boring work, explore options faster, and sometimes get you through the ugly first draft of something that would otherwise take too long to start.

Why that mattered to me

I like systems, customization, and reducing friction. So once I saw AI being used in ways that were actually practical, it clicked immediately. It was not about novelty anymore. It was about workflow.

That is why automation ended up being such a natural next step. Once you realize a model can help with real decisions, summaries, planning, or repetitive tasks, the obvious question becomes: what can I build around this so it is useful every day?

What keeps it interesting

AI is still messy. It makes mistakes, gets things wrong confidently, and often needs more structure than people expect. But honestly, that is part of why it stays interesting. It is not finished. There is still room to experiment, tune, compare models, build workflows around them, and figure out what is actually worth keeping.

For me, that is the fun part: not just using AI as a consumer, but shaping it into something that fits my own workflow, my own interests, and the kinds of problems I actually want solved.
