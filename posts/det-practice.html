Building my own DET practice setup instead of guessing

Why I made the process more technical so it would feel less random

I do better when preparation feels concrete. If something important is coming up, I would rather build a system around it than rely on vague advice and hope I somehow improve by accident. That was basically my approach to the Duolingo English Test.

Instead of only watching tips and trying random practice questions, I built a React-based practice setup for myself. The point was not to make something fancy. The point was to turn preparation into something repeatable, measurable, and easier to return to every day.

What I needed

I wanted one place where I could practice, reduce friction, and stay in rhythm. When prep feels scattered, it is easy to waste time switching between tabs, prompts, videos, and notes. A simple custom tool fixed that by making practice feel like a workflow instead of a mess.

Why building it helped

The tool itself mattered, but the bigger benefit was psychological. Once I had a system, the test stopped feeling vague. I knew what I was working on, what still felt weak, and how to keep improving without burning energy on unnecessary chaos.

The result

I ended up scoring 120 overall. That score matters, obviously, but what I like even more is that the process matched how I naturally work: build the environment, remove friction, practice inside a structure, then improve from there.
