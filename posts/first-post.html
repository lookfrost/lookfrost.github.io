How I built an automated morning brief on my home server

A real first post, based on an actual system I built

I wanted something genuinely useful, not just another demo that looks interesting for five minutes and then gets forgotten. The idea was simple: every morning, I wanted a short brief with the weather in Casablanca, tech news, gaming news, good Steam deals, and a personalized note that actually felt relevant.

The system ended up running through OpenClaw on my home server, with the final message sent through WhatsApp. On paper that sounds straightforward. In practice, it turned into one of those projects where the real work was making everything dependable instead of merely possible.

What I wanted from it

I did not want spam, noise, or a generic summary. I wanted a morning brief that felt like it was built around my actual interests: useful weather, interesting tech updates, gaming news I would care about, and Steam deals that were anchored to what I already wanted instead of random discounts on games I would never play.

That last part mattered more than it sounds. A lot of automated feeds are technically correct but personally useless. I wanted something selective. If a system is going to show up every morning, it should earn that spot by being genuinely relevant.

Where it got annoying

The hardest part was not the idea. It was reliability. I had to deal with cron setup problems, PATH issues around npm, corrupted installs, gateway token errors, and the general mess that happens when something technically works once but refuses to behave consistently after that.

That is the part I actually enjoy, even if it is frustrating in the moment. You stop guessing, isolate variables, test assumptions, break things on purpose, fix them again, and slowly move from "kind of working" to "I can trust this."

What the system actually did

The brief pulled together several things that I would otherwise check separately: Casablanca weather, current tech news, gaming news, Steam deals that matched what I already care about, and a small personalized section that made the whole thing feel less generic.

What I liked most was not any single part by itself. It was the feeling of turning scattered information into one clean ritual. Instead of opening five apps and ten tabs, I could just look at one message and move on.

Why it mattered

When the system finally worked properly, it felt different from finishing a normal small project. It was useful. It removed friction. It proved that an AI-driven workflow could do something real for me every day instead of just looking impressive in a screenshot.

That is probably what keeps pulling me toward this kind of work in general: Linux, automation, self-hosting, tuning systems, and solving technical problems until they become clean enough to disappear into daily life.

If I keep writing here, this is probably the kind of post the blog will have more of: not polished corporate-style case studies, but real systems, real frustrations, and the payoff when they finally click into place.
